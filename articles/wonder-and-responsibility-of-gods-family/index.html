The Wonder and Responsibility of Being Part of God’s Family

Joshua M. Haub · June 5, 2026

Jesus told Peter:

“Truly I say to you, there is no one who has left house or brothers or sisters or mother or father or children or farms, for My sake and for the gospel’s sake, but that he will receive a hundred times as much now in the present age, houses and brothers and sisters and mothers and children and farms, along with persecutions; and in the age to come, eternal life.” (Mark 10:29–30, NASB95)

Earlier in Mark, Jesus and a rich man (often called the “rich young ruler”) discussed what one needed to do to be saved. Jesus knew what he needed: sell all his stuff. Why did he need to do that? Because his stuff was #1 in his life. Jesus identified what was “king” in his life and told him, “You need to put Me first.” The man was unwilling to do that. On the other hand, Peter tells Jesus, “We have left everything to follow You.” Jesus then tells Peter that those who give up all receive both one hundred times more now and also eternal life.

The disciples really had left everything to follow him. Peter, for instance, had a wife and worked as a fisherman. He left that for a period of time to journey around Israel for Jesus. What do we need to give up today? Whatever it takes to make Jesus #1 in our lives. Sometimes that means we give up time, resources, family, careers, etc. We give whatever it takes to make Jesus our #1 priority.

There is great blessing that comes from following Jesus, even though the cost is so great. One of those blessings struck me when I was reading this week: We receive a spiritual family.

It is amazing that no matter where you go in the United States (and many other countries), there are Christians who we share a common bond with, who love us and will take care of us at a moment’s notice. At times I have thought about how sad and scary it must be for a person who is not a Christian to move across the country. If you do not have any previously established connections, you are literally moving to a place where you know zero people. And if you run into problems or difficulties? Good luck finding a kind neighbor, coworker, or person you met at the gym to help you. That is terrifying. It is a blessing to be part of a worldwide family.

But do we act like a family? Do we act as a congregation and/or as the (universal) family of God? What Jesus said in Mark 10 is supposed to be a blessing, but are we living as God has called us to? Are we living as a family?

Paul talks about the responsibility that the local church family in Thessalonica had to one another:

1. Family loves

“…and may the Lord cause you to increase and abound in love for one another, and for all people, just as we also do for you” (1 Thess. 3:12, NASB95)

The first thing Paul says is that the family of God loves each other. He did not say “are nice to each other.” Sometimes it is easy for a congregation to become full of pleasant people—people who come to church a few times a week, treat each other kindly (smile, wave, hug). Those are good things, but Paul does not say “be nice.” He says “love.” That is deeper. Notice what else he says: “Increase” and “Abound.”

Increase. The Greek lexicon “BDAG” notes that this idea of “increase” is like “Become rich.” It is easy for us to become lax in our love. The idea here is to continue to grow and gain more—to become so full of it we become rich.

Abound. There are several different ways this Greek word is translated, here are three ways: “Overflowing” (2 Cor. 8:12, 9:12; Col. 2:7), “More than enough” (Luke 15:17), and “Excel” (1 Thess. 4:1, 9–10). What if our love was “overflowing”? When I think of the word “overflowing,” I think of a child pouring too much water into their cup. The water cannot be contained by the cup after a certain point. What if we had so much love for one another that it could not be contained? What if our love was “more than enough”? What if every time there was a need in the church, there was a mass of people who wanted to help? That would be awesome! What if our love was “exceling”? In 4:9–10, Paul says that the Thessalonians were already loving but needed to “excel still more.” Love more and more.

2. Family comforts

“Therefore comfort one another with these words” (1 Thess. 4:18, NASB95)

The next thing Paul says is that God’s family comforts one another. In 1 Thess. 4:13–17, Paul discusses what will happen when Jesus returns: the dead Christians will be raised and then those who are alive will be caught up. Paul wanted the Thessalonians to comfort each other with the message that all Christians, alive and dead, will one day be with Jesus. There is hope for all Christians!

How are we doing with comforting each other? There are so many Christians going through various difficulties, some of which are specifically dealing with the death of loved ones who were Christians. Are we actively comforting and helping those mourning and hurting? Or are we choosing to stand by and let others do the work instead?

3. Family encourages and builds up

“Therefore encourage one another and build up one another, just as you also are doing” (1 Thess. 5:11, NASB95)

The third thing Paul says God’s family needs to do is encourage and build up each other. In 5:1–10, Paul talks about Jesus coming back and our need to be ready for that day. Therefore, encourage and build each other up—make sure you and others are ready.

As God’s family, are we taking care of one another and watching out for others’ souls? It is our responsibility as family to look out for one another. Sometimes that means we need to have difficult conversations with each other. Sometimes that means visiting those struggling within the congregation, building them up, and lifting their spirits to keep pushing forward. God’s family watches out for each other.

4. Family appreciates and loves leaders

“But we request of you, brethren, that you appreciate those who diligently labor among you, and have charge over you in the Lord and give you instruction, and that you esteem them very highly in love because of their work. Live in peace with one another” (1 Thess. 5:12–13, NASB95)

The fourth thing Paul says is that God’s family is supposed to appreciate those in leadership positions. There is a tendency sometimes to want to fight against leadership. Instead of fighting, Paul says to appreciate them and esteem them very highly in love. He ends verse 13 telling us to live in peace with each other, which is directly applicable—live in peace with your leaders. God’s family needs to stand united and if we have problems with one another, we must work them out. We cannot allow anger and division to boil amongst the family of God. Siblings fighting is not a good thing.

5. Family helps each other

“We urge you, brethren, admonish the unruly, encourage the fainthearted, help the weak, be patient with everyone” (1 Thess. 5:14, NASB95)

The last thing Paul says is that God’s family needs to help each other. Are there unruly (undisciplined) people in God’s family? Are there fainthearted? Are there weak? Yes. Whose responsibility is it to help those people? Yours. Paul just mentioned in verses 12–13 that there were leaders who were working among the church in Thessalonica. However, notice he does not say that they are the only people who are to be working! In each verse quoted above, the message from Paul has been that all those in the church in Thessalonica are to be at work in the family of God. We all have a responsibility in God’s family.

Whose job is it to work in God’s family, to continue to uphold and to help others in a local congregation? Everyone! We may be tempted to think that the work needs to be done by everyone else—“Nobody is treating so-and-so like family” or “Nobody is treating me like family.” If you are part of the local congregation, you are part of the family and share in the responsibility of making the group a true family. Family loves, comforts, encourages, builds up, appreciates, and helps others. In the family, whose responsibility is it to do these things? All of ours. We all have a part to play. May we all fulfill our part of the work to make sure we are living and acting as a family in Christ.

To Him be the Glory.

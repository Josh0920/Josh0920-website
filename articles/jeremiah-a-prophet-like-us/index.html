Jeremiah — A Prophet Like Us

Joshua M. Haub · May 13, 2023

Jeremiah is the weeping prophet. He did not want the job. He complained about it openly. He cursed the day he was born. And yet he kept going — because he could not do otherwise.

In chapter 15 he cries out:

"Woe to me, my mother, that you have borne me as a man of strife and a man of contention to all the land!" (Jeremiah 15:10)

He has done nothing wrong, and everyone hates him for it. He is the most faithful man in Jerusalem, and the most despised. God's response is not to remove the difficulty — it is to call Jeremiah to repent of his complaining and keep going.

Then in chapter 20, after Pashhur the priest has had Jeremiah beaten and put in the stocks, the lament reaches its lowest point. Jeremiah says he has become a laughingstock, that the word of the Lord has brought him reproach and derision all day long. And then he says he tried to stop:

"But if I say, 'I will not remember Him or speak anymore in His name,' then in my heart it becomes like a burning fire shut up in my bones; and I am weary of holding it in, and I cannot endure it." (Jeremiah 20:9)

He tried to quit. He decided he would not speak anymore. And he could not do it. The word was a fire inside him — contained, it burned hotter. He had to speak.

Jeremiah is a prophet like us. He struggled, doubted, complained, wanted out. And God used him anyway — kept him going, kept the word burning. The question for us is the same one Jeremiah faced in his lowest moments:

Will we keep proclaiming?

To Him Be the Glory.

Joshua M. Haub · May 13, 2023

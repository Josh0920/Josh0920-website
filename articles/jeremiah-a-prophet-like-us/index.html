Jeremiah, a Prophet Like Us

Joshua M. Haub · May 13, 2023

One of the deepest moving characters in all the Bible is Jeremiah. He was a man selected by God to prophecy to Judah before and during the time when the Babylonians came and conquered the land. There are two sections in Jeremiah where his humanity cries out to God in pain: 15:10–18 and 20:7–18.

God answers the first cry of Jeremiah because of the pain that came with being His prophet. Jeremiah questions why God is doing all of this to him (“this is all Your fault, God!” Cries the prophet). He feels the weight and severity of public rejection. Jeremiah evidently wishes he could just give up. Jeremiah blames God for all his pain.

In 15:19–21, God essentially tells Jeremiah to repent and get back to preaching and He’d be with him. In our eyes, perhaps a little bit harsh on God’s part.

God wasn’t going to let Jeremiah whip out so easily. He was going to have to continue to endure.

In chapter 20, Jeremiah cries out again. This time, he says one of the most powerful and moving statements.

In verse 7–8, Jeremiah is feeling the weight of persecution and rejection that came with being God’s prophet.

Verse 9: “But if I say, ‘I will not remember Him or speak anymore in His name,’ then in my heart it becomes like a burning fire shut up in my bones; and I am weary of holding it in, and I cannot endure it” NASB1995).

See to Jeremiah, even if he wanted to give up (which he did!), he couldn’t. The message of God was like a fire. He couldn’t contain it even if he tried. And if he did try, it would destroy him.

I wonder what would happen if the Gospel was that way to us. No matter how much persecution we go through; no matter how badly the world, friends, family, co-workers, bosses, etc; no matter how badly we are treated here in this life; if we lived with the mentality: “I just have to teach the gospel. I can’t hold it in! No matter what…” I wonder, what would our lives begin to look like?

See for Jeremiah, it didn’t matter how badly he hurt. He had to preach the message. There was no other option. If he tried to quit, his life would fall apart.

God has given us a message. Do we really think we can give up on evangelism and the message not burn within us? And if we could really give up and it not burn within us, would that not indicate we really don’t believe in it? Our lack of concern would reflect a deeper problem within our own hearts. Our silence would then become self-evident.

Jeremiah leaves us with a self-reflecting question: Will we hold it in, or will we proclaim it no matter the consequences?

To God be the glory.

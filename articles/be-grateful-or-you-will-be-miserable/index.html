Be Grateful or You Will Be Miserable

Joshua M. Haub · May 30, 2026

When you think about the story of Jonah, what often comes to mind? Probably the great fish. But think about this: “What made God vomit in the story of Jonah?”

“‘But I will sacrifice to You with the voice of thanksgiving. That which I have vowed I will pay. Salvation is from the Lord.’” (Jonah 2:9, NASB95)

What made God vomit Jonah out of the stomach of the fish (2:10)? Jonah’s decision to be both grateful and obedient. And actually, it was Jonah’s gratefulness for God’s salvation that led him to do what he vowed, which is probably a reference to Jonah now being willing to go preach to Nineveh (chapter 3).

Looking at gratitude in the book of Jonah is ironic. Jonah might be the most ungrateful prophet in the Old Testament. In chapter one, God gives him a command to preach to Nineveh, and instead of obeying, he flees in the opposite direction! God sends a great storm on the boat, but Jonah is asleep and does not pray. He does not turn to the Lord at all. He tells the sailors to throw him overboard and he begins drowning, but God sends the great fish to save him. In chapter 2, while Jonah is in the fish’s stomach, he prays, thanking God for saving him. His prayer recounts what he was going through while drowning. He prayed “from the depth of Sheol” (2:2), “point of death” (2:5), “weeds… wrapped around his head” (2:5), and “I was fainting away” (2:7). This is not good! And yet, from his lowest point (literally, the bottom of the sea), he prays and God saves him. If we were Jonah, I think we would all be saying with him, “But I will sacrifice to You with the voice of thanksgiving. That which I have vowed I will pay. Salvation is from the Lord” (2:9).

Jonah is now resolved to obey. God vomits Jonah (2:10), and he goes to Nineveh to preach the message: “Forty days and Nineveh will be destroyed.” When the people hear him, they repent. Everyone fasts, mourns, puts on sackcloth, and turns away from their evil (3:5–8). The king hopes that God will forgive them (3:9). God does (3:10).

If you were Jonah, how would you feel? Probably pretty happy! God saved you when you should have received punishment, and now God is saving Nineveh too. God extends the same kindness to Nineveh that you received.

How does Jonah react? He’s angry that God would be so kind to them and wants God to kill him (4:1–3). Seriously?! Jonah becomes bitter and resentful; turns against God and others, and is unable to believe how God could be so kind to such horrible people. He also becomes selfish. What makes Jonah happy is not that people are saved, it is that God gives him good things, like a plant for shade (4:6).

What do we call people who are bitter, resentful, angry with God and others? What do we call people who are selfish? Miserable.

Resentful and bitter. Have you ever met someone like that? What are they always talking and thinking about? How awful their lives are. Everything around them becomes bad. Their lives are bad, their family is bad, their church is bad, their government is bad. To them, society is falling apart. They say, “Everything else (or everyone else) is the issue.” What happens when you try to help them see some of the good things in their lives? They shut you down. We choose what we focus on. If you do not want to see the good things, you won’t. The opposite is true too.

Angry with God and others. Bitter and resentful people often are angry with someone: God or man. Jonah is grateful that God saved his “life” (2:6), but then he wants God to take it (4:3). He no longer treasures what God has given him. Jonah wants to die because he did not get what he wanted, Nineveh’s destruction.

Have you ever met someone who thought God was not doing what He should? Or that other people were getting what they did not deserve? They disregard their blessings from God, forget about them, and then focus on everyone else. It makes them angry that God could be so good to others when they did not deserve it. It is ironic for Jonah, and us, because God had given Jonah the same thing He gave to Nineveh — salvation and a second chance.

Selfish. Jonah is very thankful when he receives things from God. When he gets salvation (2:9), he is thankful. When he gets a plant (4:6), he is thankful. But when Nineveh gets something good from God, he bitterly says, “I cannot believe You, God! How could you do this?!” Have you ever met someone who loved when good things happened to them but hated when they happened to others? When we refuse to acknowledge and be grateful for the blessings in our lives, we will always be frustrated when others are blessed too. I do not think Jonah was “jealous” of Nineveh, but sometimes we are.

People who live life like this are miserable, not necessarily because their life is “bad” (sometimes it is), but because they only focus on what they think is bad in it. Life is full of good and bad. When we disregard the good things, we are left only with the bad. No wonder we are angry that “everyone else” is being blessed! That is not a good place to be. When we forget what God has done for us, we are left with an “unblessed life” that only has suffering.

So what should we do? Be grateful. Do not forget what God has done for you. He saved you and has given you blessings. Think about those things. Remember that gratitude motivates good obedience. If we are ungrateful, we will be poor servants of God. Be grateful or you will be miserable.

To Him be the Glory.

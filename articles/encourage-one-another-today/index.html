Reflection

Encourage One Another “Today”

Joshua M. Haub · May 15, 2026

“But encourage one another day after day, as long as it is still called ‘Today,’ so that none of you will be hardened by the deceitfulness of sin.” (Hebrews 3:13, NASB95)

In Hebrews 3, the Hebrew writer calls his audience to be people who encourage each other “Today.” Why? So that they will not fall away by having a hard heart. What is the significance of encouragement, “Today,” and hardening one’s heart?

Encouragement.

The Hebrew author is calling his audience to be people who are selfless. One cannot be an encourager while simultaneously thinking about him/herself. If all I do is think, “What will make me happy, feel good, etc.?” then I will not be very concerned with how others are doing in their lives. If my mind is filled with me, there is not much room for others. Selflessness, then, is part of what is means to encourage. Philippians 2:3–4 is a helpful text here:

“Do nothing from selfishness or empty conceit, but with humility of mind regard one another as more important than yourselves; do not merely look out for your own personal interests, but also for the interest of others.”

While Paul in Phil 2 is directing his attention to the need for selfless servitude which leads to congregational unity (i.e., the mind of Christ), what he says is a helpful reminder: we need to be putting others’ needs above our own. This is what we are doing when we are encouraging others.

But why are we encouraging others? Because how we treat our fellow Christians impacts their hearts and spiritual well-being. Consider if the verse was flipped: “But if you do not encourage one another day after day, as long as it is still called ‘Today,’ some of you will be hardened by the deceitfulness of sin.” The help we receive from one another in the face of the difficulties of life has real impact. You need that help and so do others. Do not forget that fact on the days you are doing really well; others might not be. If we are selfless and others’ focused, we will be real encouragers. Do not refuse to give to others what you yourself need. Be a helper not just a receiver.

“Today.”

What does the Hebrew writer mean when he says, “Today”? Well, firstly, we might say that we ought to encourage each other daily. “Today,” if you will. This is why the writer says, “day after day.” However, there is a more specific reason why he uses the specific word “Today.” It’s a citation from Psalm 95. In the Psalm, David (cf. Heb 4:7) writes, “Today, if you would hear His voice” (95:7). What does David mean, “Today”? He means, “Right now.” Psalms were meant to be sung, prayed, and used in worship. As this psalm was used in worship, the idea of “Today” would ring true every time the psalm was used. Thus, there is a new opportunity to listen to God. “Today,” then, is a never-ending invitation to obedience. This is precisely the Hebrew writer’s point: if David wrote “Today” in Psalm 95 for those of his generation, how could the Hebrew writer quote it and say, “You should encourage one another ‘Today’”? The “Today” of David’s day is long gone! The Hebrew writer can say “Today,” quoting Psalm 95, because the invitation is still open! As long as we are alive, we have opportunity to listen to God.

Hardened by the deceitfulness of sin.

The hardening of our hearts is a grave danger.

Notice Heb 3:18–19:

“And to whom did He [God] swear that they [the Israelites] would not enter His rest, but to those who were disobedient? So we see that they were not able to enter because of unbelief.”

Then he says, in 4:1–3:

“Therefore, let us fear if, while a promise remains of entering His rest, any one of you may seem to have come short of it. For indeed we have had good news preached to us, just as they also; but the word they heard did not profit them, because it was not united by faith in those who heard. For we who have believed enter that rest, just as He has said, ‘As I swore in My wrath, they shall not enter My rest.’”

What happens when we harden our hearts? Two things: #1. We won’t listen to God’s word. #2. We become unable to enter God’s rest (Heaven, cf. 4:9). Sin lies to us. It wants us. We cannot give in.

Encouragement, then, helps us remain softened because we are being reminded that we are not alone and that there is “rest” on the horizon. “How do I encourage others?” one may ask. Read Psalm 95. Notice anything odd about it? The Hebrew writer quotes 95:7c–11 but left out v. 1–7b. What do those verses say? Psalm 95 is a psalm of praise and worship. It emphasizes “Us,” “We,” and “Our.” It is a psalm about worshiping together. That is one way to remind and encourage one another to keep our hearts in tune with God despite the difficulties of life and the deceitfulness of sin.

So, how seriously are we taking the call to be a daily encourager, intently worshiping with the heart to build one another up to maintain faith despite the evil forces that try to make us fall away? Are you focused on self or on helping others? God is calling us to do more than just maintain our own faith, we are to look out into our church-family and see who else needs encouragement and help. We are in this together.

To Him be the Glory.

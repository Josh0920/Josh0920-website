Reflection

Make Good Friends and Be One Too

Joshua M. Haub · May 22, 2026

The Bible has a lot to say about friendship. One of the first major characters in the Bible is Abraham, “the friend of God” (James 2:23, see also 2 Chron 20:7; and Isaiah 41:8 where God Himself says Abraham is His friend). Jesus even says that we are His friends if we obey Him (John 15:14). “Friend” is a frequent topic in the New Testament (see one example: Luke 11:5–13). One of the most well-known friendships in the Bible is David and Jonathan.

There are many different kinds of “friendships” people form. Sometimes you become friends with a superior at work, someone at church, on a sports team, or at an event. There are many different dynamics that are involved in forming a relationship with someone else. But what does it mean to be a “good” friend?

The book of Proverbs has some helpful things to say about what it means to be a good friend:

1. Friends Love

“A friend loves at all times, And a brother is born for adversity.” (Proverbs 17:17)

What does that mean for a friend to “love at all times”? Our culture is infatuated with “love,” often using the word as a masked way of saying “accept me for who I am no matter what.” This is not the way the Bible speaks of love.

It may be that Solomon put the emphasis in this verse on the second part: family is so important (or maybe close, family-like friends) when life gets hard. Whatever the case, the first part of the verse is still significant. It seems to me that what Solomon is saying in the first part is that friends love and are there for you during difficult times. Solomon talks about “fake” friends in Prov 19:6 (“Many will seek the favor of a generous man, and every man is a friend to him who gives gifts.” See also 19:4). Not everyone who wants to be around you cares about you as much as they may care about what they can get from you. A person who really cares for you is not fair-weather. A good friend truly loves you and is there to help in any way they can.

2. Friends Speak Up

“Faithful are the wounds of a friend,
But deceitful are the kisses of an enemy.” (Proverbs 27:6)

One of the most difficult parts of being a real friend is correcting others when they are wrong. Perhaps the most difficult part of it is that we do not want to hurt their feelings. Hurt feelings in our culture is almost always a negative thing—something to be avoided. However, Solomon does not agree with that. He says the wounds of a friend are “faithful.” I really like the way the New English Translation of the Septuagint (NETS) translates this verse: “More trustworthy are the wounds of a friend than the spontaneous kisses of an enemy.” When a friend corrects you, you can trust them. You know they care about you and want to help. They are speaking up because they care more about if you are right with God than if you will like them afterwards.

Solomon, in the second half of the verse, drives home the idea that it is our enemies who are often the ones flattering us. Sometimes those who are boosting our egos are actually the ones who do not care about us. Our culture has this verse backwards: The friend “kisses” and the enemy “wounds.”

The longer you think about this proverb, the truer it becomes. We all know that those who are willing to correct us are the ones who really care. There may be some who are, of course, correcting us from a bad heart, but we can often identify the difference. Proverbs teaches the necessity of correction, and those who are wise desire it (see Prov 9:8–9, 12:1, 15, 15:5, 17:20). Fools hate correction (1:7, 9:8). We are called to be wise, not fools. If we work at it, we can even reach the point of saying their counsel is “sweet” (27:9).

3. Friends Have Your Back

“A man who flatters his neighbor is spreading a net for his steps.” (Proverbs 29:5)

Flattery is destructive. If we “overdo the compliments,” we are actually setting our friends up for failure. By praising, validating, and proclaiming, “You’re right!” to everything they say no matter what, as the world says to do, we are not really a good friend. A good friend watches what they say, both positive and negative. Why? Because they care about you and your soul. They recognize the dangers of both extremes and try to treat you as they would want to be treated (cf. Matt 7:12).

The world is full of bad friends. There are countless stories of “friends” who end up ruining others’ lives because they did not live like Proverbs says. They will tempt you into joining in their wickedness (Prov 1:10–14). They will lead you down a never-ending path of constant validation and misery that can only be solved if you continue to give into “your heart’s desire.” If we are not careful, we can ruin our lives too by not heeding Solomon’s instructions. Be close to people who truly love you, not just when it is easy. Be close to people who correct you when you are wrong, who care more about you than about if you will like them after. Be close to those who do not just say what you want to hear or praise you over and over again, but who are truly looking out for your soul. Find those people and befriend them. If you look for “yes-men,” you will be miserable and your life will be difficult. Just ask Ahab who was killed because he disregarded Micaiah in favor of the four hundred “yes-men” prophets (1 Kings 22:13).

Make good friends and be one too.

To Him Be the Glory.

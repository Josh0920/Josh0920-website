God Won’t Save Someone Like Me

Joshua M. Haub · March 15, 2024

People say it in different ways. "I've done too much." "I've been away too long." "You don't know what I've done." The sentiment underneath is always the same: God might save others, but not someone like me.

Paul answers this directly — not with a general theological argument, but with his own story:

"I thank Christ Jesus our Lord, who has strengthened me, because He considered me faithful, putting me into service, even though I was formerly a blasphemer and a persecutor and a violent aggressor. Yet I was shown mercy because I acted ignorantly in unbelief." (1 Timothy 1:12–13)

Blasphemer. Persecutor. Violent aggressor. Paul is not softening his past. He dragged Christians from their homes. He stood by approvingly at Stephen's stoning. He breathed threats and murder against the disciples of the Lord. By any human measure, he was an enemy of God.

And then he says this:

"Yet for this reason I found mercy, so that in me as the foremost, Jesus Christ might demonstrate His perfect patience as an example for those who would believe in Him for eternal life." (1 Timothy 1:16)

Paul was saved as an example. His conversion is a demonstration — a proof case — for everyone who comes after him. The logic is direct: if Christ can save the foremost of sinners, then no one who comes to Him is beyond saving.

That is not a loophole. That is the point. God's mercy is not strained by your past. It is not exhausted by what you have done. Paul's story exists specifically so that people who think they are too far gone would have evidence that they are not.

You are never too bad to be saved.

To Him Be the Glory.

Joshua M. Haub · March 15, 2024

Reflection

Spiritual Demases

Joshua M. Haub · March 18, 2023

The sad reality of “Spiritual Demases.”

There is a man only mentioned 3 times in Scriptures. He is mentioned in Colossians, Philemon, and 2 Timothy. His name is Demas. Demas was one of the men mentioned as being with Paul and being a worker in the Kingdom with him. Demas did a noble work for the Kingdom in serving the apostle Paul. Perhaps he did local work for the brethren in Rome as Paul was under house arrest. Perhaps he went to different places for Paul’s sake or the Gospel’s. No matter what Demas did, he was a well-respected worker in God’s kingdom in the eyes of Paul and those around him.

Demas is mentioned in Colossians 4:14 as sending greetings to the brethren at Colossae. In light of the context of this verse, it appears that Demas was a Gentile believer, not a previous Jew (though he could have been a proselyte; this fact is unknown).

Demas is mentioned in Philemon v23 as being one of Paul’s “fellow workers.” Demas worked in the same group as men like Luke and Mark, 2 of the 4 gospel writers. This would have been an incredible experience for a disciple to work with men whom the Holy Spirit picked to write about the life of Jesus.

Yet, in his final mention in the Bible, Paul writes in 2 Timothy 4:9–10 for Timothy to come to him quickly because Demas had deserted Paul. Why did Demas desert Paul? Didn’t he serve alongside men like Mark, Luke, and Paul?

Perhaps in the eyes of some, the faith of those around a certain individual ought to be strong enough to keep that one afloat.

“If everyone else around me is doing well, that ought to mean that I am doing well myself.”

Another way of saying this:

“If I just hangout with the right people, then I’ll be good. Maybe I can ‘blend in’ and be considered ‘good enough.’”

However, that is far from the truth. Perhaps we treat church like this. Maybe one would say, “As long as I go to church, then I’ll be good.” The problem with these ideas is that fundamentally, our faithfulness is not built on others (or at least, it ought not be). Our faithfulness needs to be built on our own devotion to God.

The problem with Demas was not that he was around ‘the wrong people.’ The problem was not that Mark, Luke, Paul, or the Roman brethren did not encourage him enough; the problem was his own heart. The sickness of selfishness kills the seed of the Word in the hearts of all who allow this weed to grow unattended.

Demas fell into the trap of the Thorny Soil. Ironically, Luke’s gospel gives us an in-depth look into this soil. In Luke 8:14, Jesus tells us that those who are in the “Thorny Soil Category” are the ones who become Christians, yet allow the “worries and riches and pleasures of this life” to choke out the Word “and bear no fruit to maturity” (NASB1995).

The lesson from the Thorny Soil: if we allow this world to pull us away, we will not bear fruit for Christ. If we leave weeds unattended, they will grow. You cannot live with one foot in Christ and one in the world (remember Matthew 6:24). You must choose whom you will serve.

Demas began by serving Christ; but, he ultimately chose to serve himself.

The story of Demas is a sad one. In just 3 verses, one can see a man fall to the corruption of his own heart in which he had failed to pull out the improper desires that he had allowed to grow in the place where the Word should have been thriving. Our hearts only have so much soil, so you must choose what you allow to grow.

Perhaps another reason the story of Demas is so sad is because we see other “Demases” in our lives. There are spiritual Demases everywhere. People leave Christ for all sorts of reason. One of the fundamental (or most common) reasons people leave Christ is because they have “[loved] this present world” (2 Timothy 4:10).

When these people leave Christ, we often are shocked. The reason we are shocked a lot of times is because we can’t see people’s hearts. We can only see what people allow us to. So, when people we love fall away, we are devastated. We may be able to see early signs of un-tilled weeds, but the final sign will be their actual falling away. This is a hard hit on our compassionate hearts.

Our next thought is often: “Why? Why did they leave? What changed? I thought…”

Perhaps this is the saddest part. The truth sometimes is that these desires for the world were never pulled out in the first place.

**I am not saying they were never True Christians. I believe people who fall away are oftentimes authentic Christians before their fall. But, sadly, some of the desires for the world aren’t always dealt with fully. There are cases where for a time their heart is truly “clean,” but those who fall away have allowed lustful desires to grow; either they have allowed desires to continue to grow since the beginning, or they have allowed them to be replanted and thrive.

Part of the sadness of others falling away is that we then begin wondering, “If only I had known.” Or, “If only I had seen the signs.” Or, perhaps more self-detrimental, “If only I had stepped up and done something, maybe they wouldn’t have fallen away.”

The truth: each one of us picks what kind of soil our heart will be. This is a dual-edged sword. This truth gives relief: “I can choose to serve God!” But it also means that we can choose to not be the Good Soil. Perhaps you could have done something… perhaps you could not have… but again, they chose for themselves; you cannot choose for them.

The Scriptures never tell us if Demas ever came back to the faith. Perhaps that is intentional. When people desert the faith, we are left with unanswered questions. Some people may even die leaving behind these unanswered questions about the authenticity of their faith.

“Did they really fall away, or will I see them in Heaven?”

The lesson of Demas: you may be Demas, having unattended weeds. If that’s you, deal with them before they become so big you allow them to kill the Word that is planted in your heart. If you know someone who is a Demas, look to encourage them. Be that brother who brings them back (James 5:19–20). The word of God is powerful. It is able to do remarkable things.

Seeing people become a Demas is very discouraging. It may even do harm to your faith. It may leave you broken with unanswered questions… but remember the words of the Hebrew writer,

“Therefore, since we have so great a cloud of witnesses surrounding us, let us also lay aside every encumbrance and the sin which so easily entangles us, and let us run with endurance the race that is set before us, fixing our eyes on Jesus, the author and perfecter of faith, who for the joy set before Him endured the cross, despising the shame, and has sat down at the right hand of the throne of God.” (Hebrews 12:1–2)

To God be the glory.

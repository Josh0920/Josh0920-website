Reflection

Philippians 1

Joshua M. Haub · August 17, 2024

Paul is in prison when he writes Philippians. Chained to a Roman guard, facing an uncertain future, with no guaranteed release. And the letter overflows with joy.

He tells the Philippians that his imprisonment has actually advanced the gospel — the whole praetorian guard has heard about Christ, and brothers have grown bolder in their witness. Even the difficult circumstances have served the mission.

Then he arrives at a statement that compresses an entire theology of life into a single line:

"For to me, to live is Christ and to die is gain." (Philippians 1:21)

To live is Christ. Not to live is to pursue Christ, or to follow Christ, or to try to be like Christ. To live is Christ. The living itself is Christ. Paul is not the subject of his own life anymore.

Galatians 2:20 fills in the backstory: "I have been crucified with Christ; and it is no longer I who live, but Christ lives in me." The old Paul — the one defined by his own ambitions, credentials, and identity — died. What remains is Christ living through him.

That is why death is gain. If your life is Christ, then dying just means more of Him, without limitation, without the friction of the flesh. Death is not loss for someone whose life is already not their own.

The question this raises is honest and uncomfortable: what does it actually look like to live so that Christ is the whole of it? Not 80%. Not most of it. All of it.

What does 100% for Christ look like in your ordinary life?

To Him Be the Glory.

Joshua M. Haub · August 17, 2024

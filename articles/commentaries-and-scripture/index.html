Reflection

Commentaries and Scripture

Joshua M. Haub · June 13, 2025

Commentaries are useful. I use them. I am grateful for scholars who have spent their lives in the text and left us their work. But there is a danger that comes with relying on them too heavily, and it is not a new danger.

The Pharisees were the foremost biblical scholars of their day. They had memorized the Torah. They had traditions and commentaries going back generations. And Jesus said to them:

"You invalidated the word of God for the sake of your tradition." (Matthew 15:6)

The tradition had grown so authoritative that it began to override the very Word it was meant to explain. That is the risk. It is not a risk limited to first-century Pharisees.

When we go to a commentary before we go to the text, we are shaping our reading before we have done any reading. When we trust a commentator's conclusions more than we trust our own careful study of the passage, we have let a man's word sit above God's Word.

The Bereans offer the better model:

"Now these were more noble-minded than those in Thessalonica, for they received the word with great eagerness, examining the Scriptures daily to see whether these things were so." (Acts 17:11)

They received Paul's preaching eagerly — and then went and checked it against Scripture. Not to be difficult. Not because they doubted Paul personally. Because the Scripture is the standard, and every human voice, however gifted, is measured against it.

Use commentaries. Use them as tools. Just never let a tool become the authority.

To Him Be the Glory.

Be Anxious for Nothing

Joshua M. Haub · August 3, 2024

“Be anxious for nothing… whatever is true…”

I love the book of Philippians. In ch. 4, Paul resumes his discussion on unity, which was the Philippians’ big problem. Paul tells 2 women in v. 2 to “live in harmony in the Lord.” That, to me, sets up the rest of the section (v. 3-9). I want to focus on v. 6 and 8.

1. “Be anxious for nothing” (v. 6). What does Paul mean? What he’s been saying, in the context, are things that the Philippians need to exercise in their pursuit of unity. Why mention anxiety? Well, I believe what he’s getting at is this: “Don’t get so worked up about trivial things.” The word “Anxious” here can also mean “be (unduly) concerned” (BDAG).

What are some trivial things Christians tend to get worked up about that don’t really matter? Perhaps I will ask it like this: Could we sometimes get too wrapped up in the clothes people wear to church (not speaking of immodesty)? Could we sometimes get too wrapped up in if someone “stole our seats”? Could we sometimes get annoyed that people want to change how many songs we sing each service, how long the lesson is, if Bible class is before morning worship, if we meet on Tuesday or Wednesday or Thursday?

“OH I CANNOT BELIEVE THEM!” What have we just done? We’ve violated Phil 4:6. Don’t get so worried about things that don’t matter! What we are doing, in fact, is causing disunity and being selfish.

Instead, pray “with thanksgiving” (v. 6). How much more peaceful and unified our lives would be with others if we prayed more with thankfulness instead of causing disunity.

2. “Whatever is true…dwell on these things” (v. 8). What are some things we do that causes disunity? Lie, gossip, make up stuff about others, etc. Paul, in verse 8, is countering that kind of attitude!

What would our congregations look like if instead of exaggerating and creating false accusations and gossiping (“Well, *name* is always causing problems! They are ALWAYS doing *blank*”), we did what Paul said in verse 8?

“Whatever is true” — “You know, I really like how well *name* is at helping others”
“Whatever is honorable” — “You know, I think *name* is really good at leading our thoughts in worship. That’s really helpful”
“Whatever is right” — “You know, they may not do everything right, but I really love how good they are at *blank*”

The list goes on. Instead of focusing on the lies, gossip, exaggerations, we ought to look at the GOOD and focus on being unified and selfless.

What we may find, in our pursuit of love and unity, is that our brethren are actually more Christ-like than we may believed!

If we worked as hard as we could to follow Phil 4:1-9, how much less drama would there be, and how much more joy we would have when we gather with the saints each first day of the week!

To Him be the Glory.

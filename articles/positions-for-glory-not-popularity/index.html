Positions for Glory, not for Popularity

Joshua M. Haub · February 17, 2024

The older I have gotten, the more opportunities I’ve had to be in situations where I am either given a Title or Authority, or both. Recently, I’ve been blessed with the opportunity to run as an Officer in a Club here at school. I may or may not be voted into that position, but I am hope that I am. Being in such a situation has caused me to reflect upon the attitude I need to have when it comes to obtaining Titles and Authority.

My mind immediately goes to Jesus and the 12. The 12 fought over having authority/high positions in the Kingdom. Jesus constantly shot down their desire for High Positions by telling them that to be great, they must become the “most-servant.” This is part of the Upside Truth of the Kingdom. The Kingdom looks nothing like the World’s attitude towards Status.

“Then the mother of the sons of Zebedee came to Jesus with her sons, bowing down and making a request of Him. And He said to her, ‘What do you wish?’ She said to Him, ‘Command that in Your kingdom these two sons of mine may sit one on Your right and one on Your left.’ But Jesus answered, ‘You do not know what you are asking. Are you able to drink the cup that I am about to drink?’ They said to Him, ‘We are able.’ He said to them, ‘My cup you shall drink; but to sit on My right and on My left, this is not Mine to give, but it is for those for whom it has been prepared by My Father.’ And hearing this, the ten became indignant with the two brothers. But Jesus called them to Himself and said, ‘You know that the rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their great men exercise authority over them. It is not this way among you, but whoever wishes to become great among you shall be your servant, and whoever wishes to be first among you shall be your slave; just as the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give His life a ransom for many’” (Matthew 20:20–28, NASB1995).

Jesus encountered a situation in which the mother of the sons of Zebedee desired for her sons to become Jesus’ “Right and Left hand men” in the Kingdom. To sit at Jesus’ right and left meant to be put above the rest of the 12. When the other apostles found out the sons of Zebedee were trying to surpass them in Kingdom Status, they were very angry.

Why were they angry? Perhaps for 2 reasons: #1 — the sons of Zebedee weren’t “greater” than the rest. #2 — all of the others thought they deserved to be seated at either the Right or Left hand of Jesus. Their anger was selfishly motivated.

I think often times, our attitude towards Status and Authority is much the same, whether that is within the Kingdom or communities where we live. We want Power. We want Fame. We want others to See Us. We “clout chase,” to use modern slang.

High positions are meant to be filled by those who will best fulfill that specific role. Yet, they’ve often times become tools for personal gain and Pride.

Jesus spoke of 2 Kingdoms: The World and God’s Kingdom.

Jesus says that the Gentiles (by application, Worldly people) use their status in the World to command and exercise authority over others. In God’s Kingdom, when we abuse our power for selfish gain, we are acting like the World, not like God’s servants.

How worldly are we really?

Notice again what Jesus said…

“‘It is not this way among you…’” (v 26a)

While the world uses positions for personal gain and Pride, Jesus says that God’s Kingdom and citizens look to be servants.

We must, under all circumstances, use our “Kingdom status” as means to be servants. The more status we have, the more ability we have to serve. That is powerful in God’s Kingdom.

Our goal is to Glorify God, not ourselves.

If our goal in the Kingdom becomes about self-glorification, we must step down, lest we smear our Pride and selfishness over His ordained roles.

Christ is our example: forget trying to be great and learn to be humble. Humility is great power in God’s Kingdom.

Why do we desire Power, Status, Position, and Title? Is it for God, or is it really for us?

To Him be the Glory.

Joshua M. Haub · February 17, 2024
